\section*{1. Introduction}

Chess has a clear performance metric (ELO rating~\hyperlink{ref3}{[3]}), a well-defined state space, and a definitive AI disruption timeline: AlphaZero's self-taught mastery in 2017~\hyperlink{ref1}{[1]}, followed by Stockfish NNUE in 2020~\hyperlink{ref2}{[2]}. AlphaZero's unconventional style - favoring positional advantage over material, re-evaluating centuries of opening theory - sparked a revolution across all skill levels.

This project asks whether observing superior AI play actually changed human behavior. Did players reduce blunders using post-game engine analysis? Did opening trends shift toward engine-preferred lines? Did game duration change? Can we quantify skill signals from move quality alone? We collected 200,000 games from the Lichess open database~\hyperlink{ref4}{[4]} and designed seven interactive sections, each targeting one facet of the AI-human chess relationship. Six sections explore opening trends, blunder rates, game length, and piece placement through interactive data visualizations. The seventh integrates supervised regression as a single-game rating-signal demo from ACPL, connecting data analysis directly to the user's own play without claiming to measure true rating.

The complete repository is available at \url{https://github.com/duongphamminhdung/COMP4010_Project_2}, and the website is live at \url{https://duongphamminhdung.github.io/COMP4010_Project_2/}.


\section*{2. Dataset and Preprocessing}

\textbf{Source.}
The Lichess open database~\hyperlink{ref4}{[4]} contains billions of rated games in PGN format with player ELOs, move sequences, engine evaluations, and opening classifications.

\textbf{Sampling.}
We sample $\sim$200k games across four time periods and six ELO brackets (Table~1), yielding $\sim$8,333 games per cell. Only games with engine evaluation annotations are selected. The four periods are chosen around major AI milestones so that each era represents a different stage of human access to engine knowledge.

\textbf{Era definitions.}
Pre-AI (2015-16) is the baseline before neural chess ideas became widely visible. Early Post-AI (2018-19) captures the period after AlphaZero demonstrated superhuman self-play in December 2017 and influenced public discussion of opening and positional style. NNUE Era (2021-22) follows Stockfish's adoption of neural-network evaluation in 2020, when strong engine analysis became more accessible and human-readable. Modern (2024-25) reflects current online play, where engine review, opening databases, streaming, and personalized training are routine parts of chess learning.

\textbf{Pipeline.}
Raw zstd-compressed PGN files (10-30 GB/month) are processed in two phases:
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.25em, topsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{Fast scan:} Stream PGN files, reject games lacking \texttt{\%eval} (skips 95-98\% instantly), bucket by ELO and period.
  \item \textbf{Full parse:} Parse $\sim$200k collected games with \texttt{python-chess}~\hyperlink{ref5}{[5]}, extracting eval scores, material counts, piece types, destination squares, captures, checks, sacrifices, and game metadata.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Output.}
Five raw CSVs (game metadata, moves, blunders, piece-squares, sacrifices) are aggregated into frontend-ready CSVs and JSONs ($<$3 MB total). Material and sacrifice data are loaded for the Shiny edition and remain available for future frontend sections.

\section*{3. Visualization Design}

\textbf{Section 1 - Opening Tree.}
Opening moves form a \emph{hierarchical} branching structure. We use a D3~\hyperlink{ref6}{[6]} sunburst partition chart because its concentric rings naturally encode tree depth (ply number) while arc width encodes move popularity, letting readers compare branch sizes at a glance. The sunburst also makes the key finding - that post-AI trees are thinner at deeper levels - immediately visible through narrowing arc widths.

\textbf{Section 2 - Opening Revolution.}
Opening popularity over time is \emph{temporal} data with multiple categories. A multi-line chart lets users compare trends across openings simultaneously on shared time axes, revealing that no single opening dominated or collapsed after AI - the distribution became slightly more even. Reference lines at 2017 (AlphaZero) and 2020 (NNUE) anchor the temporal context.

\textbf{Section 3 - Opening Simulator.}
Static descriptions of openings (``Sicilian Defense: 1.e4 c5'') are opaque to casual players. We use an interactive board with move-by-move playback because openings are defined by \emph{sequences of moves}, not isolated positions. Auto-play and a clickable move list give both passive and active exploration modes, translating abstract names into concrete piece plans.

\textbf{Section 4 - Blunder Heatmap.}
Blunder rates across six ELO brackets and four eras form a \emph{two-dimensional categorical} grid. We use a D3 heatmap with a diverging color scale (green $\to$ yellow $\to$ red) because color intensity lets the eye detect the diagonal gradient - the project's strongest finding - perceptually immediately. This pattern, showing that middle-rated players improved the most, would require careful serial scanning in a numerical table.

\textbf{Section 5 - Game Length Distribution.}
Game duration is a \emph{continuous distribution}, so we use normalized area curves to preserve the full distribution shape. The curves reveal a broad concentration around plies 45-60 followed by a long right tail, while their heavy overlap shows that the overall shape changed little across eras. Normalizing each era independently makes these small distribution shifts visually comparable despite unequal sample sizes.

\textbf{Section 6 - Piece-Square Maps.}
Piece placement data is inherently \emph{spatial}: the only natural canvas is the chess board itself. We overlay destination-square frequencies directly onto an 8$\times$8 board grid using a green gradient, preserving spatial meaning. Readers immediately recognize that knights cluster on c3/f3/c6/f6 - natural development squares - and that these hot spots tighten in later eras, revealing convergence on engine-approved placement.

\textbf{Section 7 - Predict ELO demo.}
This section integrates machine learning into the visualization. Users play White against a lightweight $\sim$1300 ELO bot. After each user move the board is re-evaluated; the difference between the best available evaluation and the post-move evaluation is recorded as the centipawn loss for that move. Once the user has played $\geq$20 moves, the mean centipawn loss (ACPL) is fed into a regression model fitted on our data:
\[
  \text{ELO} = 9637 - 1708 \cdot \ln(\text{ACPL}_{\text{cp}})
\]
The point estimate is then converted to an illustrative distribution over six ELO brackets via a Gaussian kernel, displayed as horizontal bars. Because one short game against a simple bot is extremely noisy, the interface frames the output as a rating signal rather than a true ELO prediction. Presenting probabilities rather than a single number teaches the reader that single-game prediction is inherently uncertain - a point estimate would falsely imply precision. Details of the ML pipeline are in Section 4.


\section*{4. Methods}

\textbf{Engine evaluations as ground truth.}
Each move carries a Stockfish~\hyperlink{ref2}{[2]} \texttt{\%eval} annotation (centipawns), serving as an objective quality metric. Eval drop between consecutive moves quantifies mistakes; we threshold at 200cp for blunders.

\textbf{Aggregation.}
Move-level data is aggregated by period (four eras around AI milestones) and ELO bracket (six bins). Year-based aggregations provide finer temporal resolution, filtered to ELO 1500+ to reduce noise.

\textbf{Tree pruning.}
Opening trees are pruned at build time: top 5 children at depths 0-2, top 3 + ``Other'' at depths 3-4, top 2 at depth 5+. This reduces JSON files from 6-7 MB to $\sim$700 KB each.


\subsection*{4.1 Predict ELO Pipeline}

The rating-signal section combines a chess bot, a regression model trained on historical data, and a probabilistic output layer. We describe each stage below.

\textbf{Bot evaluation.}
The in-browser opponent queries the Lichess cloud evaluation API, which returns Stockfish principal variations at depth 12 for a given FEN position. To simulate $\sim$1300 ELO play, the bot requests up to 4 PVs, skips the top-ranked move (too strong for amateur-level play), and selects uniformly from the remaining PVs. If the API is unavailable (offline use, rate limiting), a local minimax search with alpha-beta pruning at depth 2 and a hand-crafted evaluation function (material values: P=1, N=B=3, R=5, Q=9, K=100; plus piece-square tables) serves as fallback. The API also provides the Stockfish position eval, which is used to compute ACPL during play so that the evaluation quality matches the training annotations.

\textbf{Feature extraction during play.}
After each user move, the board is evaluated before and after the move. The centipawn loss is:
\[
  \ell_t = \max\!\bigl(0,\; v_{\text{best}} - v_{\text{after}}\bigr)
\]
where $v_{\text{best}}$ is the maximum evaluation over all legal moves and $v_{\text{after}}$ is the evaluation after the chosen move.

The key metric is \textbf{ACPL} (Average Centipawn Loss)~\hyperlink{ref7}{[7],}\hyperlink{ref8}{[8]} - the mean of $\ell_t$ over all user moves in the game:
\[
  \text{ACPL} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \ell_t
\]
ACPL measures how far the user's moves deviate from the best available move, on average. A grandmaster might have ACPL $\approx$ 10-20 (nearly optimal play), while a beginner might have ACPL $\approx$ 150-300 (frequent large mistakes). This metric is well-established in chess analytics: Guid \& Bratko~\hyperlink{ref7}{[7]} used it to compare world champions, and Regan \& Haworth~\hyperlink{ref8}{[8]} showed it correlates strongly with ELO rating across skill levels.

Additional behavioral features are computed during play for display and for compatibility with the exported multi-feature model, but the active rating signal uses ACPL only. These features include: \textbf{blunder rate} ($|\{\ell_t > 200\text{cp}\}|/n$), \textbf{capture percentage}, \textbf{check percentage}, \textbf{piece diversity} (unique piece types moved / 6), and \textbf{center control} (moves to the 16 central squares / $n$).

\textbf{Regression models.}
Training data was prepared by aggregating the $\sim$200k games to the player-game level (295,603 records), computing ACPL and behavioral features for each player's side of a single game. Two models were fitted:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.45em, topsep=0.35em]
  \item \textbf{ACPL-only model} (active in the frontend): A logarithmic regression $\text{ELO} = a - b \cdot \ln(\text{ACPL}_{\text{cp}})$, fitted on binned averages (one data point per ELO bracket, requiring $\geq$20 games per bin). Final parameters: $a = 9637$, $b = 1708$. The model captures the monotonic relationship between lower ACPL and higher ELO but has poor explanatory power on raw individual data ($R^2 < 0$ on the full dataset), reflecting the inherent noise of predicting long-term rating from a single game.

  \item \textbf{Multi-feature model} (trained, parameters exported): A linear regression on four standardized features - $\ln(\text{ACPL})$, blunder rate, capture percentage, and check percentage - fitted with feature standardization. Each feature $x_i$ is transformed as $(x_i - \mu_i) / s_i$ before the weighted sum:
  \[
    \text{ELO} = 1795 - 93 \cdot \hat{x}_{\ln\text{ACPL}} - 227 \cdot \hat{x}_{\text{blunder}} - 135 \cdot \hat{x}_{\text{capture}} - 26 \cdot \hat{x}_{\text{check}}
  \]
  This model achieves $R^2 = 0.31$, explaining 31\% of ELO variance across player-games. Blunder rate has the largest coefficient ($-$227), confirming that move-losing blunders are the strongest single predictor of rating after ACPL itself.

  Despite better fit, the ACPL-only model is used in the frontend because the Gaussian output layer already smooths prediction uncertainty, and the simpler formula avoids feature-scaling overhead on every move.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Probabilistic output.}
The model score $\hat{E}$ is converted to illustrative bracket signal bars via a Gaussian kernel:
\[
  P(\text{bracket}_j) = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(-\frac{(\hat{E} - c_j)^2}{2\sigma^2}\bigr)}{\sum_{k} \exp\!\bigl(-\frac{(\hat{E} - c_k)^2}{2\sigma^2}\bigr)}, \quad \sigma = 300
\]
where $c_j$ are bracket centers (500, 1200, 1600, 2000, 2400, 2800). This soft assignment avoids hard classification boundaries, but it should not be read as calibrated confidence in a real rating. It visualizes where one game's ACPL-like signal falls relative to population-level bracket centers.

\textbf{Game-result prior.}
Defeating the $\sim$1300 bot sets a floor: $\hat{E} \leftarrow \max(\hat{E}, 1400)$. Losing caps the estimate: $\hat{E} \leftarrow \min(\hat{E}, 1500)$. This incorporates the intuition that a player who wins against a known-strength opponent is unlikely to be rated below them.

\textbf{How ML contributes to the analysis.}
The rating-signal section serves three roles. First, it \emph{validates} the dataset: the $R^2 = 0.31$ of the multi-feature model confirms that move quality does carry real signal about player strength, even if single-game noise limits precision. Second, it \emph{demonstrates} the ACPL-ELO relationship interactively: users experience the correlation firsthand rather than trusting a static chart. Third, it \emph{communicates uncertainty honestly}: by presenting bracket-like signal bars and an explicit caveat, the visualization teaches users that prediction from one game is inherently imprecise.


\section*{5. Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=0.55em, topsep=0.35em]
  \item \textbf{AI did not replace classical opening structure.} The main first-move split, especially 1.e4 versus 1.d4, remains stable across eras. The change appears deeper in the tree: later-era branches become narrower, suggesting that players increasingly converge on a smaller set of trusted engine-approved continuations.

  \item \textbf{Opening trends changed by evolution, not revolution.} No major opening suddenly disappeared after AlphaZero or NNUE. Instead, popularity shifts are gradual: the Sicilian remains central, the Queen's Gambit gains visibility, and the Italian becomes more prominent again as modern engines validate classical development plans.

  \item \textbf{The clearest AI-era improvement is fewer blunders.} Blunder rates decline across every rating bracket, with the largest gains in the 1400-2200 range. This suggests that intermediate and advanced club players benefit most from accessible post-game engine analysis, while elite players improve less because their baseline accuracy was already high.

  \item \textbf{Game length changed only slightly across eras.} Most games end between roughly 45 and 60 ply, followed by a long tail of less frequent extended games. Median length falls from 65 ply before AI to 62-63 ply in the later eras, but the curves overlap heavily. The data therefore supports a modest shortening rather than a fundamental change in game duration.

  \item \textbf{Piece placement stayed spatially stable, but became more concentrated.} The same natural development squares dominate in every era: knights cluster on c3/f3/c6/f6, while bishops often appear on active diagonals and fianchetto squares. The stronger change is not location but concentration, with later eras showing tighter hot spots around efficient development squares.

  \item \textbf{Move quality contains rating signal, but not enough for certainty.} Across the full dataset, stronger players lose fewer centipawns per move, and the multi-feature model explains a meaningful share of rating variation ($R^2 = 0.31$). However, one game is too noisy for precise individual prediction, so the interactive ELO section correctly frames its output as a rating signal rather than a true rating estimate.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the strongest conclusion is that AI changed \emph{how} players learn more than \emph{what} chess is. Human play became more accurate and more concentrated around trusted ideas, but the core geometry of openings, piece placement, and game flow remained recognizable.


\section*{6. Challenges, Limitations, and Future Work}

\textbf{Data challenges.}
The Lichess database is much larger than what we could download or process fully within the project timeline. A single monthly PGN file is already 10-30 GB compressed, and the full archive contains many years of games, so downloading every month and parsing every game was not realistic. Processing was also expensive because PGN is a sequential text format: each game must be streamed, decompressed, checked for engine annotations, parsed into moves, and converted into board states before features such as eval drops, captures, checks, piece locations, and openings can be extracted. Most games do not contain \texttt{\%eval} annotations, so parsing them fully would waste a large amount of time.

To make the pipeline feasible, we used a two-stage strategy: first, a fast scan rejected games without engine evaluations and filled balanced period-ELO buckets; second, only the sampled games were fully parsed with \texttt{python-chess}. Even with this reduction, preprocessing required careful memory control and long runtime. Raw opening trees also became too large for the web interface (160k-178k nodes), so heuristic pruning was necessary to keep the visualization responsive. The modern era (2024-25) has fewer available months than earlier eras, making its opening tree naturally sparser.

\textbf{Design challenges.}
Choosing the right visualization for each data type required iteration. Early prototypes used tree diagrams for openings (unreadable at scale), bar charts for blunder rates (hard to compare across cells), and raw game-length counts that made eras difficult to compare because their sample sizes differed. The current sunburst, heatmap, and normalized distribution designs emerged from testing each against the question it needed to answer.

\textbf{ML limitations.}
The ACPL-only model's negative $R^2$ on raw data highlights that a single game's ACPL is an extremely noisy proxy for long-term rating; the multi-feature model ($R^2 = 0.31$) is better but still far from precise. The bot has fixed strength ($\sim$1300), so the game-result prior is uninformative for players rated far above or below that level. The local fallback evaluation uses piece-square tables and depth-2 search, which is a coarse approximation of true engine evaluation; however, the primary API path uses Stockfish depth 12, matching the evaluation quality of the training annotations.

\textbf{General limitations.}
Lichess ELO is not directly comparable to FIDE~\hyperlink{ref9}{[9]} and has experienced inflation. Only games with engine evaluations are included (selection bias toward engaged players). Game-length differences may reflect changes in time-control mix, resignation habits, or online playing culture rather than AI exposure alone. This is observational; we cannot prove causation - other factors (the pandemic-era chess boom~\hyperlink{ref10}{[10]}, streaming culture) likely contribute.

\textbf{Future work.}
Move-by-move blunder timelines (opening vs.\ middlegame vs.\ endgame phases), clock-time analysis for blitz/bullet games, text search in the sunburst chart, per-opening blunder rates (combining opening and blunder datasets), using the multi-feature model to make the rating-signal demo less noisy while keeping the caveat explicit, and lazy-loading of opening tree JSONs for faster page load.
